Title: Federal Reserve Raises Interest Rates by 25 Basis Points

The Federal Reserve announced on Wednesday that it would raise its benchmark interest rate by 25 basis points, bringing the federal funds rate to a target range of 5.25% to 5.50%. This marks the eleventh rate hike since March 2022, as the central bank continues its aggressive campaign to bring inflation down to its 2% target. Fed Chair Jerome Powell stated that while inflation has eased significantly from its peak of 9.1% in June 2022, it remains above the desired level, and the committee remains committed to achieving price stability.

The decision was unanimous among the 12 voting members of the Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC). Markets had widely anticipated the move, with futures traders pricing in a 97% probability of the hike ahead of the announcement. However, uncertainty remains about whether this will be the final rate increase of the current tightening cycle. Powell declined to rule out additional hikes, saying decisions would be made on a meeting-by-meeting basis depending on incoming economic data.

The impact of rising rates has already been felt across various sectors. Mortgage rates have climbed above 7% for the first time in more than two decades, cooling the once red-hot housing market. Consumer credit card rates have also surged, putting pressure on household finances. Meanwhile, the banking sector has faced stress, with regional banks struggling to manage the mismatch between short-term deposits and long-term assets. Despite these pressures, the labor market has remained resilient, with unemployment holding near historic lows at 3.6%. Analysts warn that the full effects of monetary tightening are still working their way through the economy, and a soft landing remains the goal but is not guaranteed.

=== ARTICLE 2 ===
Title: Apple Reports Record Quarterly Revenue Driven by iPhone Sales

Apple Inc. reported record quarterly revenue of $117.2 billion for its fiscal first quarter, beating analyst expectations and driven primarily by strong iPhone sales, particularly the iPhone 15 Pro and Pro Max models. The company's net income rose to $33.9 billion, compared to $29.9 billion in the same quarter last year, reflecting a 13.4% year-over-year increase. Chief Executive Tim Cook credited the strong performance to robust demand across all major product categories and geographic regions, including a surprising recovery in China despite ongoing macroeconomic headwinds.

Services revenue reached an all-time high of $23.1 billion, surpassing Apple's hardware segments in growth rate with a 16.3% increase year-over-year. The services division, which includes the App Store, Apple Music, Apple TV+, iCloud, and Apple Pay, now accounts for nearly 20% of total revenue and carries significantly higher profit margins than hardware products. Chief Financial Officer Luca Maestri highlighted that the installed base of active Apple devices has surpassed 2.2 billion units worldwide, providing a growing ecosystem that supports recurring services revenue.

Mac and iPad revenues were mixed, with Mac sales declining 27% due to the previous year's high base effect following a product refresh cycle, while iPad revenue grew modestly by 3%. Wearables, Home and Accessories — which includes Apple Watch, AirPods, and HomePod — declined by 11% amid a broader slowdown in consumer electronics spending. Looking ahead, Apple issued cautious guidance for the upcoming quarter, citing foreign exchange headwinds and macroeconomic uncertainty. Despite this, analysts remain broadly bullish on Apple's long-term prospects, pointing to its deep customer loyalty, expanding services ecosystem, and strong balance sheet with over $165 billion in cash and investments.

=== ARTICLE 3 ===
Title: Oil Prices Surge as OPEC+ Announces Surprise Production Cut

Crude oil prices surged more than 6% on Monday after the OPEC+ alliance announced a surprise production cut of 1.16 million barrels per day, effective from May through the end of 2023. The decision, led by Saudi Arabia, caught global energy markets off guard as most analysts had expected the group to maintain current output levels. Brent crude jumped to $84.93 per barrel, while West Texas Intermediate (WTI) rose to $80.42 per barrel following the announcement. The move was seen as a strategic effort by OPEC+ to support oil prices amid concerns about weakening global demand and a slowdown in major economies.

The production cut drew sharp criticism from the United States, with the White House stating it was "not advisable" given the current state of the global economy and inflationary pressures still facing consumers worldwide. U.S. energy officials expressed concern that higher oil prices could reverse progress made in reducing inflation and potentially trigger renewed economic slowdown. Meanwhile, energy analysts at Goldman Sachs revised their year-end Brent crude forecast upward to $95 per barrel, citing the unexpected supply constraint and anticipated demand recovery from China as the world's second-largest economy rebounds from its COVID-19 restrictions.

The ripple effects of the production cut were felt across global financial markets. Energy stocks rallied strongly, with ExxonMobil gaining 4.2% and Chevron rising 3.8% in early trading. Airline stocks fell sharply as investors anticipated higher jet fuel costs, with Delta Airlines dropping 4.5% and United Airlines declining 3.9%. Economists warned that sustained higher oil prices could complicate the Federal Reserve's fight against inflation, potentially forcing additional interest rate hikes. Consumers in the United States and Europe are likely to feel the impact at the gas pump within weeks, with average U.S. gasoline prices forecast to rise by 20 to 30 cents per gallon in the near term.

=== ARTICLE 4 ===
Title: Global Stock Markets Tumble Amid Banking Sector Fears

Global stock markets experienced their worst single-day decline in over a year on Friday, as fears of a broader banking sector crisis spread following the sudden collapse of two mid-sized U.S. regional banks. Silicon Valley Bank (SVB), once the 16th largest bank in the United States with assets of $209 billion, was shut down by California regulators after a bank run triggered by concerns about its investment portfolio losses. Hours later, Signature Bank, a New York-based lender with significant exposure to the cryptocurrency sector, was also closed by regulators. The combined collapse of the two institutions represented the largest bank failures since the 2008 financial crisis.

The S&P 500 fell 1.8%, the Dow Jones Industrial Average dropped 1.1%, and the tech-heavy Nasdaq Composite declined 2.1% on the day. European markets were also hit hard, with Germany's DAX falling 3.1% and France's CAC 40 dropping 2.9%. Bank stocks bore the brunt of the selloff, with the KBW Bank Index plummeting 7.7% — its worst one-day performance since the early days of the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020. Shares of First Republic Bank, another mid-sized regional lender, fell more than 33% amid speculation about its financial stability and potential contagion risks spreading to other institutions.

The U.S. government moved swiftly to contain the panic. The Federal Reserve, Treasury Department, and Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC) issued a joint statement guaranteeing all deposits at both failed banks, going beyond the standard $250,000 FDIC insurance limit. President Biden addressed the nation, assuring Americans that the banking system was safe and that those responsible would be held accountable. The Fed also announced the creation of a new emergency lending facility called the Bank Term Funding Program (BTFP), designed to provide banks with liquidity against their bond holdings at par value. Despite these measures, analysts warned that confidence in regional banks had been severely shaken, and that tighter credit conditions and increased regulatory scrutiny were likely in the months ahead.

=== ARTICLE 5 ===
Title: India's GDP Growth Accelerates to 8.4% Making It World's Fastest Growing Major Economy

India's gross domestic product grew at an annual rate of 8.4% in the third quarter of the fiscal year, surpassing all major economist forecasts and cementing the country's position as the world's fastest-growing large economy. The strong growth was driven by robust performance in the manufacturing sector, which expanded by 11.6%, and continued momentum in construction and infrastructure spending fueled by the government's ambitious capital expenditure program. The data released by India's National Statistical Office showed that the economy has now fully recovered and exceeded its pre-pandemic trajectory, despite global headwinds including high interest rates in developed economies and sluggish demand from key export partners.

Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman hailed the figures as a testament to the strength of India's economic fundamentals and the success of government policies aimed at boosting domestic manufacturing through initiatives such as Production Linked Incentive (PLI) schemes. Foreign direct investment inflows into India reached $71 billion in the previous fiscal year, reflecting growing confidence among global investors in India's long-term growth story. The International Monetary Fund (IMF) and World Bank both revised their India growth forecasts upward, with the IMF projecting 6.8% growth for the full fiscal year — well above the global average of 3.1%.

However, economists cautioned that challenges remain. Rural consumption growth has lagged behind urban spending, reflecting persistent income inequality and the uneven distribution of economic gains. Inflation, while moderating, continues to exceed the Reserve Bank of India's 4% target, limiting the central bank's ability to cut interest rates and provide additional monetary stimulus. Unemployment among youth remains elevated, and the services export sector — a major driver of foreign exchange earnings — faces headwinds from slowing technology spending in the United States and Europe. Despite these concerns, India's demographic dividend, with a median age of just 28 years, and its growing middle class are widely seen as structural advantages that will sustain long-term economic expansion.
